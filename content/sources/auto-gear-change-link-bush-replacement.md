## Summary

This video demonstrates how to replace the automatic gear change link bush on the Land Rover Discovery 3/4 and Range Rover Sport L320. The presenters work underneath the car to access the gear linkage cable that connects to the automatic transmission. The bush inside the linkage arm seizes over time, causing the gear selector to become stiff or, in worse cases, preventing the car from being taken out of park or causing it to jump out of Drive.

The procedure involves removing two bolts (13mm socket and a nut), extracting the old arm, drifting out the worn plastic/metal insert from the arm, cleaning everything up, and reassembling. The presenters mark the cable position with paint before removal to ensure correct reinstallation. They recommend putting the car in off-road mode to raise the air suspension for better access, with an axle stand placed underneath as a safety precaution in case an airbag fails.

## Key Points

- The gear change link bush seizes over time, causing stiff gear changes
- If left unaddressed, it can prevent selecting gears entirely or cause the transmission to jump out of Drive
- Put the car in off-road mode to raise the suspension for better underbody access
- Always place an axle stand under the car in case an air suspension bag fails
- Mark the cable position with paint before removal to ensure correct reassembly
- The top bolt requires a 13mm socket; there is also a larger nut on the outside
- The insert has different shaped castellations on each end -- hemispherical on the cable side, square on the bolt head side
- The holding nut on the arm is 21mm
- Apply copper grease on reassembly
- Use a spanner to hold the arm steady to avoid stressing the gearbox internals
- After reassembly, test drive to confirm all gears engage smoothly
- This was discovered during a Land Rover recall visit (servo air system and tow bar recall)

## Parts Mentioned

- Bush insert (plastic with metal liner and castellations)
- Spring washer
- Copper grease
