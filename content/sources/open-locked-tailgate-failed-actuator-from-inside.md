## Summary

This is the final video in a three-part series on Discovery 3/4 tailgate actuator issues. It addresses the worst-case scenario: the upper tailgate actuator has failed completely and the tailgate is stuck closed. The problem is a catch-22 -- the carpet trim panel hiding the actuator is held in by plastic trim that can only be removed when the tailgate is open. The presenter and Tyler lock themselves inside the car boot and demonstrate how to break through the trim from inside to access the actuator cable and manually release the tailgate.

The method involves pulling back the carpet trim from the top edge (breaking clips if necessary), wedging it open with wooden blocks, ripping into the foam backing to expose the actuator, then using a tool and hammer to push the Bowden cable out of the actuator from behind. Once the cable is dislodged, pulling it releases the tailgate latch. The presenter estimates this takes about 20 minutes. The second half of the video provides an excellent bench demonstration of the actuator mechanism, showing how the solenoid, cable, latch, and spring clips all work together, and demonstrates the two common failure modes.

## Key Points

- The upper tailgate release actuator is located behind the lower tailgate carpet panel, which is trapped behind trim that can only be removed when the tailgate is open -- a design catch-22
- You cannot open the tailgate by removing hinges because the lower tailgate latches are also locked
- Early warning signs: the tailgate becomes increasingly difficult to open -- do not ignore this, replace the actuator immediately
- To release from inside: climb into the boot through the doors, peel back the carpet trim from the top edge, wedge it open with wooden blocks, tear through the foam backing to reach the actuator
- Use a flat tool and hammer to push the Bowden cable out of the actuator from behind
- Once the cable is dislodged, pull it to release the upper tailgate latch
- The actuator mechanism works via an electromagnet/solenoid that pulls a cable connected to the latch
- Two common failure modes:
  1. The solenoid loses strength and cannot pull hard enough to release the latch (most common, demonstrated on bench)
  2. The spring clips that hold the cable hook break, disconnecting the cable from the mechanism
- The actuator wire colors: purple and black is positive
- The latch and actuator are two separate purchasable parts -- the cable comes with the door lock assembly, not the actuator
- If you damage the cable during emergency release, you will need to buy a new lock assembly (three bolts to remove on inside)
- Consider fitting the Land Rover Passion manual release lever as a preventive measure
