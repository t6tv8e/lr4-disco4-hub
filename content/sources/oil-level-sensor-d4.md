## Summary

This video is an investigative teardown of the electronic oil level sensor found on the Discovery 4 (a change from the Discovery 3, which used a dipstick). The presenter removes the sensor from a spare sump, cuts it open, and tries to understand how it works. The video was prompted by a viewer who was quoted over 1,000 pounds by a dealer to replace a failed sensor, despite the part itself costing only around 45 pounds -- the labour is expensive because the entire sump must be removed.

The investigation reveals the sensor uses ultrasonic technology (similar to parking sensors) to measure oil level, and also incorporates a temperature sensor. The tube on the sensor is an anti-slosh device with small ribs/grooves that allow oil to gradually enter and exit, preventing erratic readings from oil sloshing in the sump. The presenter was unable to successfully bench-test the sensor to determine good/bad voltage readings, but explains the operating principle and offers practical advice.

## Key Points

- The D4 uses an electronic oil level sensor instead of a dipstick (changed from D3)
- The sensor costs approximately 45 pounds but requires sump removal to replace -- dealer quotes can exceed 1,000 pounds
- The sensor is bolted into the sump with 3x 8mm bolts and sealed with an O-ring
- It has 3 wires (positive, negative, signal) and uses ultrasonic measurement
- The tube on the sensor is an anti-slosh device -- small ribs/grooves allow oil to enter gradually for stable readings
- A cap on top acts as a breather to let air in while keeping oil from entering from above
- The oil level reading requires waiting at least 5-10 minutes after engine shutdown for the oil to settle through the narrow entrance
- Also contains a temperature sensor (how it does both on 3 wires is unclear to the presenter)
- First troubleshooting step: check and clean the external electrical connector with contact cleaner
- If buying a new sensor, also buy a new wiring lead
- There is no practical way to retrofit a dipstick to the D4 sump -- the presenter investigated and the potential entry points are above the sensor measurement range
- The sump also contains an oil pickup with a gauze filter strapped in with a cable tie
- The presenter could not determine bench-test voltage values for a known-good sensor
