## Summary

LR TIME installs the transmission, transfer case, prop shafts, and muffler on their Discovery frame-off restoration project. The key discovery during reassembly is a hidden transfer case input seal leak — found only after removing the transfer case from the transmission. A Land Rover dealer had previously quoted approximately EUR 4,000 for a full body-off, engine-out, transmission-out repair with a new ZF gearbox, but the actual fix was a single seal replacement using fabricated tools. Christian builds a seal extractor from scrap metal (ground to shape with a lip, driven out with a pull hammer) and a seal installer from an aluminum mountain bike handlebar cut to the measured depth of the original seal. The 17-year-old shift linkage showed zero play — a genuine surprise and testament to engineering quality. The ZF self-adjusting clutch throw bearing initially caused alarm (appeared badly off-center out of the box) but proved to be correctly designed to self-center on first actuation. The original GKN rear prop shaft was replaced with a Chinese aftermarket unit at EUR 139 because the center bearing had disintegrated and the boot is non-removable, making bearing-only replacement impractical.

## Notes

- This is part of an LS3-swapped Discovery rebuild series (Season 4, Episode 32)
- The frame is now fully assembled with all drivetrain components — next step is engine installation
- Land Rover specifies new prop shaft bolts but they were reused here
- Heat insulation tape (Tesa brand, furnace duct grade) was wrapped around the transmission
- Christian notes the engineering complexity of some Land Rover clips and connectors as over-engineered for field serviceability
- Temperature during work was 32 degrees C
