## Summary

LR TIME diagnoses and services a fuel burning heater on a Discovery 3 that is throwing a premature flame detection fault in 2 degrees C weather. The fault is caused by carbon buildup in the combustion chamber after approximately 5 years without service — not a sensor or electrical failure. The video explains the FBH operating logic (activates below 6 degrees C outside temperature when coolant is below 75 degrees C, shuts off at 85 degrees C), walks through the complete removal procedure (air muffler, wiring, coolant hoses, exhaust muffler, fuel line), and describes the combustion chamber cleaning process. The cleaning footage was lost, so the video references Robbie's FBH Servicing Guide on disco3.co.uk for detailed photos. After reassembly with copper paste on fittings and coolant top-up, the heater successfully started at 6 degrees C.

## Notes

- Premature flame detection fault is caused by carbon buildup, not sensor or electrical failure. The heater starts fine from cold but fails to restart after a short stop.
- FBH activation logic: outside temp below 6 degrees C AND coolant below 75 degrees C. Shuts off at 85 degrees C coolant.
- Expect approximately 0.3 liters of coolant loss during removal — top up after running the vehicle.
- Second exhaust muffler bolt requires prying back wheel arch plastic with wheels turned out for access.
- Apply WD-40 to fuel line fitting before disconnecting — expect some diesel spillage.
- Handle aged plastic latches on combustion chamber cap with extreme care — 16-year-old plastic is fragile.
- Note O-rings at coolant ports during disassembly — do not damage them.
- Apply copper paste to fittings before reinstalling.
- Refer to Robbie's FBH Servicing Guide on disco3.co.uk for detailed combustion chamber cleaning photos.
- Service interval: every few years to prevent carbon accumulation.
