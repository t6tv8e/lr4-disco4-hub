## Summary

Christian and Vera from LR TIME document a complete DIY underbody rust protection treatment on their Land Rover Discovery 3, motivated by a TUV (German roadworthiness inspection) warning that the car would fail within two years due to chassis corrosion. Previous rust protection attempts on a Freelander had been unsuccessful with consumer-grade products.

The process involved 2.5 hours of grinding the underbody using a die grinder with wire brush bits and an angle grinder, followed by brake cleaner decontamination, heating with a gas burner to remove moisture, application of a German corrosion protection primer sprayed with a 1.4mm nozzle air gun, followed by Mercedes-Benz underbody protection coating (approximately 45 EUR per bottle, used by Mercedes body shops for warranty corrosion repairs), and finally Mercedes-Benz cavity preservation wax injected into all enclosed cavities.

The key problem areas were the chassis (not the frame), particularly above the rear axle and under the spare wheel. The results were described as making the underbody look like a new vehicle. The Mercedes-Benz products were chosen because they are professional-grade, globally available through dealerships, and proven in the Mercedes-Benz warranty repair program.

## Notes

- The TUV warning was specifically about the chassis, not the frame — an important distinction for D3/D4 owners in countries with roadworthiness inspections
- The Mercedes-Benz underbody coating is water-based and has no smell — clean tools with hot water while still wet (solvents do not work)
- The Mercedes spray nozzle can spray evenly from 1.5 feet away — very effective but easy to over-apply
- Removing the electric parking brake module provides much better access for grinding and painting underneath
- Wire brush bits for die grinder need RPM adjustment to last — without it, they burn through too quickly
- A 9mm Allen wrench is needed for some underbody cover bolts — not part of standard metric sets, which go 8, 10
- The host's quality test for corrosion products: "If it doesn't kill you, it's not good" — the primer is labeled deadly if swallowed
