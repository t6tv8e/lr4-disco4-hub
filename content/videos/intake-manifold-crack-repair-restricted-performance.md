## Summary

Christian from LR TIME tackles the dreaded "Restricted Performance" issue on a Discovery 4 SDV6, one of the most common and annoying problems owners face. Using a DIY smoke generator (baby oil, wire wick, powered from the car battery), he injects smoke through the MAP sensor port to trace boost leaks.

The culprit is identified as a crack in the plastic intake manifold on the right-hand bank — a known weak spot in the casting that both manifolds can suffer from. The official repair requires removing the valve cover, which is a 10+ hour job (often done body-off) costing approximately €2,500 with high risk of causing secondary damage.

Instead, LR TIME demonstrates a "trail repair" approach: removing the rubber insulator to expose the crack, fabricating a 1mm aluminium reinforcement plate shaped to fit over the cracked area, and bonding it with two-component epoxy after proper surface preparation (40-grit sanding + acetone cleaning). The repair is presented humorously as something you could do "in the Simpson Desert" using fender panel aluminium and sandstone.

The repair works on test drive at full operating temperature, but Christian honestly notes that standard epoxy is only rated to 100°C while the manifold area reaches 150-160°C. He recommends using JB Weld for a more durable version and is also exploring plastic welding with metal crimp inserts.

## Notes

- MAP sensor port is the ideal entry point for smoke testing — easy access with O-ring seal
- Turbo bypass valve can be vacuum-actuated to test the secondary turbo path
- Smoke leaking from the air box bottom is normal — there's a membrane designed to let water out
- Taking the valve cover off a D4 is described as a 10-hour job with high risk of breaking things
- The rubber insulator over the manifold area can be removed without tools (no bolts) — careful not to rip it
