## Summary

LR TIME tears down and meticulously inspects a 270,000 km Discovery 3 TDV6 diesel engine (manufactured March 2006) to answer the question: how well does this engine actually hold up? The results are remarkably positive. All six fuel injectors came out by hand without tools after 17 years. The oil filter housing check valve was still functional. Torque-to-rotate measured only 14-15 Nm against a 25 Nm Land Rover limit, indicating the engine was still spinning freely. Conrod bearing clearances were 0.055mm (non-working) and 0.063mm (working direction) against a spec of 0.024-0.056mm -- slightly out on the thrust side but with a perfectly uniform wear pattern across all conrods. Main bearing plastigauge showed 0.038mm on all four mains against a 0.054mm limit -- right in the middle of spec, leading to the decision to keep the original bearings rather than replace with aftermarket. Pistons looked visually perfect but measured out of spec on skirt wear, leading to new Nural pistons at EUR 67 each. The key conclusion: the TDV6's known weak spots are the oil pump and crankshaft, but the combustion chamber, pistons, bearings, and oil delivery system are NOT failure points when maintained with 5W-40 oil. The 2005-2006 variant is considered the best due to minimal emission control measures.

## Notes

- Christian and Vera emphasize that 5W-40 oil is essential — this engine's condition proves it superior to 5W-30 for TDV6 longevity
- High-performance bearings are explicitly warned against — they have less embedding material than standard OEM bearings, which is wrong for long-term diesel operation
- Glow plug function can be diagnosed from piston crown burn patterns (uneven pattern = dead glow plug)
- Core plugs were corroded and required drilling out — much easier with the head removed
- The crankshaft date stamp (March 30, 2006, 16:00) prompted an extended humorous analysis of factory conditions
- This is the same Discovery 3 (Fabian's) whose engine block was later line-bored by Christian in a separate video
