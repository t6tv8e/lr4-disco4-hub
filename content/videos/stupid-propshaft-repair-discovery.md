## Summary

Christian and Vera from LR TIME diagnose a clicking/knocking noise on their Land Rover Discovery that appeared during a vacation road trip. Initial suspicion pointed at the transfer case, but a loaded low-speed test on a lift (one person in the car in gear with brakes partially applied, the other observing underneath) revealed the CV joint on their cheap Chinese aftermarket propshaft was visibly hopping — the center bearing itself was fine.

The aftermarket propshaft had cost only EUR 129 and lasted approximately 3 years / 70,000 km. With the same Chinese replacement now priced at EUR 320 and a new OEM shaft over EUR 400, they chose to rebuild their original GKN OEM propshaft by pressing in a new center bearing. This required fabricating a custom bearing puller (about an hour of work), careful heat application to free the old bearing without damaging splines, reinstalling the slinger, replacing the CV boot O-ring with an original GKN-spec part, and resealing the crimped boot cap with Deco sealant. The refurbished shaft was reinstalled and torqued (30 Nm center bearing, with a second person on the brakes for the flange bolts).

They also fixed a leaking breather tube that had caused a gear fluid smell in the cabin during their Alps trip, requiring the crossmember to be lowered for access.

## Notes

- The OEM GKN driveshaft had lasted 220,000+ km with zero play in the joints — only the center bearing rubber had failed
- Chinese aftermarket propshafts use thicker tube walls and MIG welding vs OEM magnetic arc welding — heavier, looser tolerances, shorter lifespan
- The GKN plant in Birmingham where the original shaft was manufactured has since closed
- A loaded low-speed spin test on a lift is an effective diagnostic technique for isolating driveline noise sources
