## Summary

Christian and Vera from LR TIME replace the rear propshaft on their Discovery 3 after discovering the center bearing rubber had completely disintegrated. Before removal, they mount an action camera to the transfer case to film the bearing's behaviour during normal driving — the footage clearly shows it bouncing and rubbing, yet no vibration was felt inside the cabin, even at autobahn speeds around 160 km/h. The diesel engine effectively masked the degradation.

The replacement uses a Chinese aftermarket shaft costing approximately EUR 130 (vs roughly 5x more for OEM). Installation requires a 3/8" ratchet with extension, 13mm socket, and two torque settings: 30 Nm for propshaft flange bolts and 150 Nm for the rear differential bolts (with a helper pressing the brakes). The air suspension fuse must be pulled before lifting and reinserted afterward. The aftermarket shaft had a noticeably loose fit on the rear differential — a known compromise with budget parts that is analysed in detail in the companion engineering video.

## Notes

- An underbody action camera is a clever, low-cost diagnostic tool for inspecting driveline components while the vehicle is in motion
- The center bearing failure was likely present for tens of thousands of km without any cabin symptoms
- This video is part of a multi-part series — the companion video (o70e_3QioZI) provides the detailed engineering comparison between OEM and aftermarket shafts
- The universal joint's straight-running orientation causes "notching" — localized wear that eventually creates play
