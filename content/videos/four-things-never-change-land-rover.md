## Summary

Christian and Vera present four modifications they strongly advise against on the Discovery 3 and 4. First, never remove the air suspension for coil springs: the suspension dropping is almost always a symptom of ECU limp mode from unrelated faults, not an air suspension failure. The system provides constant ride height under any load (up to 3.5-tonne trailers), drops below 1.85m for parking garages, auto-lowers above 160 km/h for stability, and raises for off-road clearance. Second, do not restyle a Discovery 3 to look like a D4 (clear tail lights, LED headlights, facelifted bumpers) as it destroys the D3's distinctive identity. Third, avoid engine remapping on the 2.7 TDV6 (190 hp) or 3.0 SDV6 (256 hp) -- no proven fuel economy, acceleration, or longevity benefit exists, and these engines are already sensitive to maintenance. Fourth, never attempt an engine swap (including LS swaps): the engine is deeply integrated with ABS, transmission control, steering, AC, vacuum pump, and CAN bus, making swaps functionally impractical. Even a TDV8 swap from a Range Rover Sport sharing the same platform was an extremely demanding build.

## Notes

- Air suspension system components: 3 valve blocks, hoses, air tank, and compressor
- Vehicle lowers below 1.85m in access height mode for German parking garages
- Auto-lowers above approximately 160 km/h, raises back below 120 km/h
- 245 hp TDV6 and 256 hp SDV6 differ only by mapping, but upgrade requires matching transmission
- One UK builder swapped a TDV8 from Range Rover Sport into a Discovery -- even with platform commonality, it was extremely demanding
- Upcoming repairs mentioned: oil pump, glow plugs, idler pulleys, timing belt (front and rear), brake light switch on Vera's D3
- Transmission flash planned for the Discovery 4
