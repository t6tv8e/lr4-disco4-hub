## Summary

Christian and Vera compare their Range Rover Sport V8 ("the Queen") with their Discovery 4 SDV6 through daily ownership. The V8's torque curve is poorly matched to real-world driving, with peak torque near 2,000 RPM leaving it unresponsive at typical urban speeds, while the SDV6 delivers instant low-end torque and better fuel economy. The V8 averaged 19 L/100 km over mixed driving versus the D4's significantly lower consumption. Ergonomically, the Range Rover's touchscreen-controlled interface (multi-step heated seat activation, massage seats that shut off every 10 minutes) compares poorly to the Discovery 4's direct-button approach. Cold-weather testing at -7 to -10 degrees shows the fuel-burning heater is highly effective but the interior heating is weaker than the Discovery's, and all doors froze shut. Running costs are high: 766 EUR for two winter tyres (22-inch), 125 EUR for a sensor, 350+ EUR for power steering hoses. The conclusion: for another Range Rover Sport, they would choose the SDV6 version and budget for the known crankshaft repair.

## Notes

- V8 fuel consumption: 19 L/100km over 300 km of mixed driving when pushed
- Discovery 4 TDV6 258 hp pulls effortlessly to 160 km/h; V8 requires constant driver input
- Heated windscreen effectively melts ice up to the exact boundary of the heated zone
- 22-inch Continental V Contact winter tyres: 766 EUR for two (no all-season with snowflake symbol available in that size)
- Discovery 4 heated seats: direct button press; Range Rover: multi-step menu process
- Air suspension auto-lowers above approximately 160 km/h on the autobahn, raises back below 120 km/h
- 20-ton hydraulic press required full body weight for suspension bushing pressing
- AGM battery in Discovery 3 requires AGM-compatible charger; standard charger can cause damage
