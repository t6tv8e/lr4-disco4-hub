## Summary

Christian and Vera from LR TIME continue their full body-off restoration of a 270,000 km Land Rover Discovery 3 manual. In this episode, they remove the TDV6 engine from the bare frame (completed in approximately 4.5 hours), inspect the drivetrain, and plan the comprehensive chassis overhaul.

The engine removal is shown to be significantly easier on the Discovery 3 manual than the Discovery 4, with the manual transmission not requiring flywheel bolt access through the starter aperture. Key findings include a worn dual mass flywheel (replaced with a new LUK unit at ~270 EUR), a clutch friction disc worn from 9.4 mm to 6.6 mm (new LUK kit at 224 EUR), a rear crankshaft seal oil leak, a completely corroded lower intercooler, cracked air shocks, corroded brake lines with a confirmed leak, and corroded parking brake cables causing the parking brake motor to jam.

A detailed parts replacement list is presented covering the clutch, DMF, brake lines, parking brake cables (60 EUR each), stabilizer bar bushes, air compressor desiccant, and air ride height sensors. The original air compressor is notably still functional after 17 years and 270,000 km. The chassis will be fully stripped, ground down, chemically treated, and repainted using German-recommended corrosion protection products.

## Notes

- Engine was well maintained and not leaking oil after 270,000 km — demonstrates longevity of the TDV6 when properly serviced
- A corroded ground stud near the starter was identified as a likely cause of many unexplained electrical issues reported on forums
- The clutch line had to be cut during removal due to corrosion — new line ordered at 68 EUR from Land Rover
- Shifter seal was leaking oil — replacement part less than 10 EUR from Land Rover
- Stabilizer bar looked terrible but wire brushing revealed the metal underneath was fine — corrosion volume is 8x the original steel volume
- The original air suspension compressor surviving 270,000 km with daily use contradicts the common internet claim that these compressors fail frequently
- Air ride height sensor bolts were all corroded and would have been impossible to remove in field conditions — freed up and reinstalled during restoration
