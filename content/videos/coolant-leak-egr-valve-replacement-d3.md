## Summary

LR TIME tackles an emergency late-afternoon repair on a Discovery 3 TDV6 with two simultaneous problems: a faulty EGR valve throwing a position sensor fault code and a major coolant leak. The EGR diagnosis is methodical — plug in the new valve, clear codes, confirm the fault stays gone, then reinstall the old valve to prove the fault returns before committing to full disassembly. The key access trick is removing the dipstick tube upper bolt to bend the EGR valve forward, exposing the otherwise inaccessible rear bolt that required a stubby ratchet wrench and took roughly an hour in real time.

The coolant leak turned out to have two sources: a hairline crack in a plastic coolant manifold and a sheared O-ring near the oil cooler, likely caused by dry installation without lubrication. Both were fixed with inexpensive parts — the replacement manifold cost 28-36 EUR and the correct O-ring was found in a 6.99 EUR assortment kit. All O-rings were reinstalled with silicone grease to prevent recurrence.

## Notes

- EGR valve swap-diagnosis method eliminates guesswork: test the new part first, confirm the old one reproduces the fault
- Dipstick tube bolt removal is a non-obvious but critical trick for EGR rear bolt access on the TDV6
- EGR bolt torque is only 10 Nm — very low; over-torquing will cause damage
- Always block the intake opening with a rag when working near it to prevent debris from entering the turbocharger
- Two different vendors sold identical-looking coolant manifolds at different prices (28 vs 36 EUR), suggesting the same factory origin
- EGR blanking is mentioned as illegal in Germany with severe consequences
