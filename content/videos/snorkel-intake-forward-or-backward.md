## Summary

Christian tests whether a snorkel should face forward (ram-air) or backward on the Discovery 3 TDV6. The key finding: a forward-facing snorkel on a turbocharged diesel disrupts the factory engine mapping by artificially pressurizing the intake, causing the ECU to inject more diesel without any power gain. After nearly a year of forward-facing driving, reversing the snorkel to face backward immediately reduced fuel consumption by approximately 1-1.5 litres per tank at highway cruising speeds. Additional benefits of backward-facing include significantly less wind noise at 140 km/h, reduced rainwater ingestion (important if the airbox drain valve is broken, a common D3 issue), and a more pleasant intake sound at low off-road speeds. The cold-air intake claim is debunked: on a turbocharged diesel with an already well-positioned intake, air temperature at snorkel height is not measurably cooler. The Bravo snorkel (180 EUR) was chosen over the Safari (500 EUR) since in Germany the snorkel is primarily cosmetic. The video also covers critical intake maintenance: checking the O-ring seal between snorkel and intake pipe for unmetered air leaks, verifying airbox lid seating after workshop visits, and MAF sensor cleaning.

## Notes

- Bravo snorkel: approximately 180 EUR; Safari snorkel: approximately 500 EUR
- K&N air filter after one year in Germany showed minimal dust -- "out of the box clean" by Australian standards
- Forward-facing snorkel would only benefit from ram-air at high speeds, and even then requires an engine remap to be useful
- Any ram-air benefit is irrelevant off-road where speeds are typically 25 km/h
- Snorkel benefits ranked: water crossing capability, dust reduction, aesthetics; cold air is a myth on this engine
- Smoke test confirmed their snorkel installation has minor leaks -- irrelevant in dry Germany but critical for water crossing
