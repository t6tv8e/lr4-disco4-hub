## Summary

Christian installs the crankshaft and pistons into a line-bored SDV6 engine block, spending the first major section demonstrating why block cleaning is the single highest-leverage step in an engine rebuild. He uses an endoscope camera to reveal debris in oil galleries that survived multiple cleaning passes, developing a systematic method using stainless steel rotary brushes, microfiber towel strips (burned edges for lint elimination), and repeated inspection camera verification until galleries are "surgically clean."

Piston cooling jets (squirters) are pressure-tested at 2 bar to verify their internal check valves function — one set from the donor engine was initially clogged, consistent with that engine having suffered a burned piston. The video covers the complete 19-stage Ford main bearing torque procedure, emphasizing its deliberately confusing alternating inner/outer bolt pattern and the critical step of pushing the crankshaft forward before torquing main bearing #4 (thrust bearing). Post-assembly checks confirm torque to rotate at 1.5 Nm (spec < 3 Nm) and crankshaft end float at 0.24 mm (spec 0.021-0.43 mm).

## Notes

- Part of the LR TIME SDV6 engine rebuild series (S6-EP07)
- The Prince Charming block was scrapped due to a crack found during cleanup honing — Jaguar block substituted
- Professional line boring in Germany: ~900 EUR, 12-week lead time, few quality shops
- Mix of King Bearing (1) and OEM bearings (3) used per side to achieve correct clearance with new crankshaft
- Assembly lube: heavy 75W-140 gear oil — functionally equivalent to expensive branded products
- New OEM crankshaft TIR must be below 0.025 mm; factory cranks typically less than hundredths
- Clean crankshaft oil passages with pipe cleaners pulled THROUGH (not back out) to avoid redepositing debris
- Christian works in the machine tool industry (production drive shaft balancers, 750K-1.5M EUR) — relevant professional background for precision machining work
