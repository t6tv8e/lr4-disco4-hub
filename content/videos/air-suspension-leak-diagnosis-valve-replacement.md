## Summary

Christian and Vera from LR TIME document the diagnosis and repair of an intermittent air suspension leak on their 2006 Discovery 3 with 260,000 km. The vehicle consistently dropped on the front left corner, triggering a suspension fault warning, but the leak was impossible to find with static testing alone.

The diagnosis began by raising the vehicle to off-road height and pulling the air suspension fuse (3rd from bottom) to lock the height. After four hours, the front left had visibly dropped, confirming an active leak on that corner. Even with both front wheel arch liners removed and soapy water applied everywhere, no leak was visible until they began actively cycling the suspension up and down using the GAP diagnostic tool — at which point visible bubbles and audible hissing appeared at a valve connector.

The 16-year-old plastic air valve was replaced with a 50 EUR Amazon aftermarket unit (OEM was unavailable due to supply issues), and the leak was confirmed fixed. The video includes extensive practical ownership advice, noting that the vehicle's original OEM air shocks are still functioning after 260,000 km and 16 years of wet German conditions — evidence that the air suspension system is more reliable than its reputation suggests when properly maintained.

## Notes

- Key insight: intermittent valve leaks come and go as the suspension cycles — debris temporarily seats O-rings, making the leak invisible during static inspection
- Neglecting air suspension problems causes cascade failures (valve leak leads to compressor failure leads to further damage)
- Regular suspension cycling (not leaving at one height for months) extends valve life by flushing debris
- A diagnostic tool is considered essential for Discovery 3 ownership — buy it before buying the vehicle
- Winter tire requirements in Germany also covered: M+S and snowflake rating required, partial fault assigned in accidents without them
