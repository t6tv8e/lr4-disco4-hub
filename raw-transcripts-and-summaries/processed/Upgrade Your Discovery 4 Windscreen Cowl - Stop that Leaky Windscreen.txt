## Replacing a Leaking Cabriolet/Convertible Soft Top Header Seal (Land Rover)

*TLDR: A step-by-step walkthrough of replacing a leaking convertible roof header/ceiling seal on a Land Rover, including wiper arm removal and refitting.*

---

### 🔧 Why the Repair Was Needed

- **Leaking soft top**: The existing header seal was letting water in, risking damage to interior components.
- **Critical electrical risk**: A **PCU (gearbox control unit)** is located beneath the header area — water ingress can prevent gear selection and render the car undriveable.

---

### 🛒 Parts & Tools

- **Replacement header seal**: Sourced from eBay for **€140**; comparable parts listed at **€300+** elsewhere.
- **Part quality**: Turned out to be a genuine **Land Rover OEM branded** part, not an aftermarket product.
- **Wiper arm puller tool**: A specialist puller tool (with threaded center bolt and arms) is strongly recommended — screwdrivers or pipe wrenches were ineffective on rusty wiper spindles.

---

### 🪛 Step-by-Step Process

- **Mark wiper positions first**: Before removing wiper arms (held by a **13mm nut**), mark their resting position on the windscreen with tape to ensure correct realignment on refitting.
- **Use a dedicated puller**: The wiper arm puller tool threads around the arm base and pulls it cleanly off the splined post — essential when corrosion is present.
- **Remove interior ceiling trim**: The ceiling panel is **clip-mounted only** — no tools needed, it unclips easily by hand.
- **Check for water/dirt accumulation**: Inspect and clean the channel where the seal sits so the new seal lies flush against the windscreen frame.
- **New seal has pre-applied adhesive**: The replacement part comes with glue already on it for windscreen contact.
- **Fit from the sides first**: Start by tucking the seal under the existing side covers (side covers do not need to be fully removed), then work across, confirming clips are seated visually and audibly.
- **Refit ceiling trim and wipers**: Clip ceiling back in, refit wiper arms to the marked positions, and test wiper operation.

---

### 💡 Bottom Line

This repair is **accessible to a home mechanic** with minimal tools, but the **wiper arm puller is non-negotiable on older/rusty cars**. The key risk — a water-damaged gearbox PCU — makes this a high-priority fix despite its low cost and relatively simple execution. Sourcing OEM parts secondhand (here at less than half retail price) is a viable and reliable option for this job.
